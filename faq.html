Frequently Asked Questions About Therapy

Common questions about starting therapy, how sessions work, insurance, telehealth, and what to expect from Hope Counseling & Family Therapy in Boulder, CO.

Getting Started

Therapy is right for anyone who wants support navigating a challenge, transition, or pattern that feels too difficult to work through alone. You don't need to be in crisis, and waiting until you are often makes things harder. Many people come to therapy during ordinary life transitions to think more clearly, break old patterns, and move forward more effectively.

Research consistently shows that the therapeutic relationship is one of the strongest predictors of therapy outcomes. In other words, how connected you feel to your therapist matters. Use the free consultation to assess whether you feel heard, respected, and comfortable. A skilled therapist who isn't right for you will produce worse outcomes than a slightly less experienced therapist who is a great fit.

It's a brief phone call, typically 15 minutes, where you share what's bringing you to therapy, ask any questions you have, and get a sense of whether I might be a good fit. No formal assessment, no pressure, no commitment required. It's just a conversation.

The first session is mostly about getting to know you: your history, what's bringing you in, what you hope to get from therapy. I'll ask questions to understand your situation. No pressure to share everything at once. We'll also talk about how we'll work together, and you're free to ask me questions too. At the end, we'll decide together whether to continue.

It varies widely by person and goal. Brief, focused work around a specific transition might take 3–6 months. Deeper work addressing longstanding patterns often takes longer. Many clients work with me for a year or more, moving to less frequent sessions as goals are met. We review progress regularly and adjust accordingly.

Couples & Family Therapy

Couples therapy can be highly effective, even for relationships in serious distress. Success depends primarily on both partners' willingness to engage honestly. Some couples use it to rebuild; others use it to separate more consciously. Both are valid outcomes.

It's common for one partner to be more reluctant. Starting with individual therapy or coaching can still shift relationship dynamics effectively. When things change at home, reluctant partners often become more willing to participate. I can advise on how to approach the invitation in a way that doesn't increase resistance.

Yes. My practice is fully affirming of all relationship structures, gender identities, and sexual orientations. I work with same-sex couples, non-traditional partnerships, and individuals across the LGBTQ+ spectrum.

My family work focuses on parent–teen pairs and family dynamics rather than individual teen therapy. If your teen needs individual support, I can provide referrals to excellent adolescent therapists in the Boulder and Denver area.

Confidentiality & Privacy

Yes. Therapy is legally protected as confidential. There are a small number of exceptions required by law, including imminent risk of harm to self or others and mandatory reporting of child or elder abuse. These are explained in the consent forms you'll complete before your first session. Beyond those legal exceptions, what you share in sessions stays in the room.

Telehealth sessions are conducted through HIPAA-compliant video platforms that meet federal privacy standards. Your session data is encrypted and protected. Standard video platforms like Zoom or FaceTime are not HIPAA-compliant and are not used for sessions.
