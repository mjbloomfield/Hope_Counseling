Adult Children & Parents in Therapy Together

For grown adults working through old family-of-origin dynamics with parents who are still in the picture, and often aging.

The Relationship That Doesn't Stop Mattering

You're an adult, and so is your parent, but the relationship has decades of history behind it and patterns laid down in childhood that keep pulling everyone back into roles that no longer fit, even when the present-day reality is very different from how things started.

This kind of family therapy is a structured space to work on the relationship itself, rather than trying to "fix mom," "fix dad," or "fix me." The focus is on the dynamic between the two of you: the misunderstandings, the things that have been left unsaid, the assumptions that drive everything, and the boundaries that either haven't been drawn or have been drawn unkindly.

Why Adults Bring This to Therapy

  • A parent's aging or health changes are reshaping the relationship in ways neither of you chose
  • Old wounds from childhood are still alive, and you'd like to address them while the parent is still here
  • Caregiving conversations are stalling out and creating resentment
  • Adult-sibling dynamics around a parent are pulling everyone backward
  • Life events like a divorce, a remarriage, or a grandchild are forcing new conversations that haven't happened yet
  • You want to build a real adult relationship with your parent before the chance is gone
  • A parent's behavior or words still have outsized power over your life, and you'd like that to change

How the Work Is Structured

Sessions usually involve the adult child and the parent together, sometimes in person and sometimes over telehealth (which can be especially useful when parents live in a different city). The work centers on the relationship between you, with both people as full participants in the conversation.

I draw on family-systems theory, attachment work, and direct relational practice. The pace is honest without being brutal, and the aim is repair where repair is possible, clarity where it isn't, and an adult-to-adult relationship that both of you can actually live with.

Individual therapy or coaching often pairs well with this kind of work. Sometimes the right next step is therapy with just you, to clarify what you actually want before bringing the parent in, and we can figure out which sequence makes sense in the consultation.

Themes That Often Come Up

Caring for elders, identity inside the family, loneliness or regret, anxiety about a parent's mortality, sexual or relational identities that have never been openly spoken about, financial concerns, and the larger legacy question of what you want to have said before the chance is gone.
