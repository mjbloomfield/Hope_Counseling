Adult Children & Parents in Therapy Together

For grown adults working through old family-of-origin dynamics with parents who are still in the picture — and often aging.

The Relationship That Doesn't Stop Mattering

You're an adult. So is your parent. The relationship has decades of history, patterns set in childhood, and a current-day reality that's very different from how it started — but the old grooves keep pulling everyone back into roles that no longer fit.

Adult-child & parent therapy is a structured space to work on the relationship itself. Not "fix mom" or "fix dad" — and not "fix me." Work on the dynamic between the two of you: the misunderstandings, the unsaid things, the assumptions, the boundaries that haven't been drawn or have been drawn unkindly.

Why Adults Bring This to Therapy

  • A parent's aging or health changes are reshaping the relationship in ways neither of you chose
  • Old wounds from childhood are still alive, and you'd like to address them while the parent is still here
  • Caregiving conversations are stalling out and creating resentment
  • Adult-sibling dynamics around a parent are pulling everyone backward
  • A divorce, a remarriage, a grandchild — life events are forcing new conversations
  • You want to build a real adult relationship with your parent before the chance is gone
  • A parent's behavior or words still have outsized power over your life, and you'd like that to change

How the Work Is Structured

Sessions usually involve the adult child and the parent together — sometimes in person, sometimes over telehealth (especially helpful when parents live in a different city). The work focuses on the relationship between you, with both people as full participants.

I draw on family-systems theory, IFS, attachment work, and direct relational practice. The pace is honest but not brutal. The goal is repair where repair is possible, clarity where it isn't, and an adult-to-adult relationship that both of you can live with.

Often individual or coaching work pairs well with this. Sometimes the right next step is therapy with just you, to clarify what you actually want before bringing the parent in. We can figure that out together in a consultation.

Themes That Often Come Up

Caring for elders, identity inside the family, loneliness or regret, anxiety about a parent's mortality, sexual or relational identities that have never been spoken about, financial concerns, the legacy question of "what do I want to have said before they're gone."
