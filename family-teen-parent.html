Teen & Parent Therapy in Boulder & Denver, CO

Family sessions designed for one parent and one teen working together on the relationship between you, rather than the all-too-common arrangement where a teenager is sent to therapy alone to be "fixed."

Why "Just Send the Teen" Doesn't Usually Work

When parents and teens are in conflict, the most common request I get is some version of "can you fix my teenager?" The patterns, though, live in the dynamic between two people rather than inside one of them, so real change tends to require both parent and teen in the room.

This kind of therapy is structured for the pair: the teen isn't the patient and the relationship is. The parent isn't a side character either; the parent is genuinely half the work. The aim is a relationship that you can both live inside honestly while the teen finishes growing up.

When This Helps

  • Communication that has degraded into shutdown, sarcasm, or repeated fights
  • A teen pulling away in a way that feels like more than normal teen distance
  • Big conflict around school, screens, friends, mental health, identity, or autonomy
  • A divorce or family change that has reshaped the parent–teen relationship
  • A repair you want to make where something said or unsaid is still in the room
  • A parent wanting to stay close as the teen launches into the next chapter

A Note About Age

I work with teens 13 and older, and I don't see kids younger than that in therapy. For families with younger children, the work happens on the family system itself, in parents of young kids therapy, with the parents (or one parent) in the room and the kids at home.

How Sessions Are Structured

Most often the parent and teen come together, weekly. Sometimes I'll meet with each of you individually for a session to check in or work on something specific, then bring you back together. The pacing is collaborative and we work out what fits as we go.

I draw on family-systems theory, attachment work, IFS, and direct relational practice. Teens are treated as full participants in the conversation, rather than as "the problem," and parents get coached in real time. Both of you are seen as people doing the best you can with what you've been handed.

Telehealth often works well for this kind of work, especially when scheduling around school and sports, or with a teen who's resistant to "going to therapy." Sometimes the screen actually makes it easier to talk.
